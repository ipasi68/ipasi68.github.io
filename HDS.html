Human Digestive Anatomy: A Closer Look

Although we are genetically close to other primates, studies suggest that humans share several physiological digestive features with canids—especially in terms of stomach acidity and gut proportions. The following scientific findings explore how our digestive system may reflect an evolutionary adaptation to omnivory and high-protein intake.

Stomach Acidity and Pathogen Defense

This study examined stomach pH across mammals and concluded that humans have highly acidic gastric conditions, comparable to scavengers. The authors suggest this adaptation is a protective mechanism against pathogens in animal-based diets.

Beasley et al., PLOS ONE, 2015

Gut Proportions in Humans vs. Primates

This anthropological source highlights how humans have a relatively small colon (~17–23%) and a larger small intestine (~56–67%), whereas great apes have the opposite pattern. This reflects a shift from fermentative to enzymatic digestion.

Milton K., Human Evolution & Digestive Strategy

Comparative Physiology with Canids

Anatomic comparisons show that humans and canids (dogs, wolves) share features such as short colons, rapid digestion, and extremely acidic gastric environments, suitable for omnivorous or carnivorous diets.

Vetericyn: The Dog Digestive System, 2023

Microbiome and Digestive Adaptation

This research reviews how microbial compositions in the human gut more closely resemble those of omnivorous mammals like canids than of herbivorous primates, supporting the hypothesis of a high-protein, animal-inclusive ancestral diet.

Comparative Gut Morphology, University of Michigan

Digestive Evolution in Homo Sapiens

This article summarizes theories that humans evolved reduced colon length and increased reliance on the small intestine as part of the shift toward energy-dense foods, especially meat and cooked starches.

Why Is Human Stomach Acid So Strong?, 2023
